One mental model before anything else

Agent, not a chatbot — and you already know how to delegate

A chatbot explains how to build a study plan. An agent builds it, schedules it, and checks on you Tuesday. Everything in this masterclass rests on a three-part model — and it maps to something nursing school already taught you: safe delegation.

Workflows

The care plan

A workflow is a written, repeatable process: what happens, in what order, and what "done" looks like. You've been writing these your whole career — they were called care plans and protocols.

Agent

The delegate

Hermes reads the plan, interprets it, and carries it out — the way you delegate to capable assistive personnel. It acts within the boundaries you set, reports back, and improves from your corrections.

Tools

The equipment

Email, files, calendars, spreadsheets, the web — the instruments the agent actually works with. Each connection is granted deliberately, like equipment checked out and signed for.

And the part that never gets delegated: accountability. You remain the licensed judgment in the loop. The agent proposes; you approve what matters. That principle — the human gate — is built into every module below, not saved for a safety lecture at the end.

In a hurry? Prove it to yourself in a week

The 7-Day Fast Track

You don't need all ten modules to feel the change. Seven days, about an hour a day, one visible win per day — each day borrows exactly what it needs from the curriculum above. At the end you have a working system and the confidence to go deeper.

Day 1

Ship your first workflow

Set up (if you haven't), load your SOUL file, and run the morning huddle — then make one correction and watch it stick. Draws on Modules 1–2.

Done when: your huddle ran, and one correction became permanent

Day 2

Connect one real source

Wire your separate AI-life Gmail or Drive. Inspect exactly what the agent can now see and do, and write down what you granted. Draws on Module 5.

Done when: one connector works, and its permissions are documented

Day 3

Make it reusable

Turn Day 1's workflow into a saved skill and run it on a second, different input. One build, many uses — that's the whole trick. Draws on Modules 4 & 6.

Done when: the same skill worked twice without re-explaining

Day 4

Make it reliable

Move your keys out of chat and into proper secrets, check the logs, and set a spending cap before you need one. Draws on Modules 2 & 8.

Done when: secrets are stored properly and a budget cap exists

Day 5

Stand up your station board

Personalize the local dashboard from your starter kit and bookmark it — your whole system, one glance, nothing uploaded anywhere. Draws on Module 7.

Done when: your dashboard opens from a bookmark with your real priorities on it

Day 6

Schedule it — with limits

Put one job on a schedule (a weekly review works beautifully), add an alert, and decide where a human — you — must approve. Draws on Modules 6 & 8.

Done when: one job runs on its own, inside limits you chose

Day 7 — the capstone

Assemble your chief of staff

Connect the pieces — email, calendar, tasks, memory, schedule — into one working assistant, and write a one-page operating guide for it: what it does daily, what it asks before doing, what it never touches. That guide is your proof you're an operator now, not a user.

Done when: the operating guide is written and tomorrow's huddle runs without you starting it

Finish a day? Tell someone. Bring your win — and one thing you'd improve — to the monthly Lamp Huddle, or share it with the community. Teaching one colleague what you built is Day 8.

The method behind every lesson

How every lesson works

Every lesson in this masterclass follows the same seven-beat pattern — the same way every good skills lab follows one. You always know where you are and what "done" means.

The real-world job

Every lesson opens with the problem it removes from your actual week — never with the technology.

One mental model

A single idea, usually borrowed from nursing, before any clicking. If you can give report, you can learn this.

A starter artifact

A prompt, checklist, file, or kit you copy instead of writing from scratch. Nobody here starts from a blank page.

A complete build

The full procedure with a visible done-state — you'll know it worked because you'll be looking at the result.

A quick self-check

A few questions to confirm the idea landed — a self-review, not a test. Nothing is graded; everything is learnable twice.

Share one win, one improvement

Say it out loud — at the Lamp Huddle, in the community, or to the colleague at the next locker. Teaching it seals it.

The bridge to the next lesson

Each lesson ends by naming exactly what the next one assumes — so you never open a page unprepared.
