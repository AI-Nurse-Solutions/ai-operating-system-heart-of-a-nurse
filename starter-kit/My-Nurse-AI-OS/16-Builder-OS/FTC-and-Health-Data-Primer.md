# The Law That Still Applies When HIPAA Doesn't — a primer for nurse entrepreneurs

*Plain language, U.S.-focused, and **not legal advice** — this is orientation so your questions to an actual attorney are good ones.*

## The misconception that catches nurses

You know HIPAA from work — so it's natural to assume it follows you into your side gig. Mostly, it doesn't. **HIPAA applies to covered entities** (providers billing electronically, health plans, clearinghouses) **and their business associates.** A nurse coaching clients directly, selling a study course, or running a wellness newsletter is usually *none of those things*.

That is not good news. It means **nothing in HIPAA protects your clients' information in your business — you are the privacy program.** And clients assume the opposite: people believe health information they give a nurse is legally protected, and your credential makes that assumption stronger. Honoring it is on you.

## What actually applies to you

**The FTC Act.** The Federal Trade Commission polices unfair and deceptive practices — including how any business collects, uses, and shares health information. And the FTC defines "health information" **broadly**: not just conditions a client tells you about, but anything that lets someone *infer* health status — what they bought, searched, or signed up for. Your coaching-client list is arguably health data by itself.

**The Health Breach Notification Rule.** Non-HIPAA apps, sites, and services that hold consumers' health information can be required to report breaches to the FTC and to the people affected. If your business keeps client health records in any form, understand this rule before you scale.

**The enforcement record is not hypothetical.** The FTC has acted against BetterHelp, GoodRx, and Premom for sharing users' health information for advertising purposes without **affirmative express consent**. The rule of thumb that keeps you far from that line: **client information is never marketing material.** Not for lookalike audiences, not for testimonials without explicit written consent, not "anonymized" lists uploaded to ad platforms.

**Your state.** Consumer-privacy and telehealth-adjacent laws vary by state and change often — one of the two questions worth an hour of an attorney's time before your first paid client (the other: whether your offer touches your licensed scope).

## The five practical rules (already wired into your Builder OS)

1. **Collect the minimum.** If the service works without knowing it, don't ask for it.
2. **Client details are session-only** in your AI system — the Do-Not-Remember entrepreneur extension enforces this. A pinned first name and context is the ceiling.
3. **Never use client information for marketing** without affirmative, express, written consent — and even then, ask yourself if you'd want it done to you.
4. **Say what you do, do what you say.** Your privacy promise (even a three-sentence one on your landing page) is an FTC-enforceable commitment. Keep it modest and true.
5. **Don't claim "HIPAA-compliant."** For a non-covered business the claim is usually meaningless — and a deceptive-practice risk. Say what's true instead: *"I collect the minimum, never sell or share your information, and never use it for advertising."*

## When to actually call a professional

Before the first paid client (scope + state law) · before storing any client health information · before any partnership that shares data · at the first whiff of a breach. An hour of counsel is cheaper than any of the alternatives.

> No PHI. No patient-specific clinical decisions. No employer secrets. No certification claim. Human judgment first.
