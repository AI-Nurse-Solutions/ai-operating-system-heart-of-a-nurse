# When an AI-Assisted Workflow Goes Wrong — the unit one-pager

Print this. The day you need it is not the day to design it. It adapts the clinical incident-response arc — detect, contain, learn — to unit-sized AI incidents: a brief that went out with an error, a draft that leaked something it shouldn't have held, a skill that did something outside its charter, a staff member harmed or alarmed by an AI-assisted process.

## First, the frame

An AI incident on a unit is a **process incident**, and you already know how to run those: no blame theater, no cover-up, fast containment, honest learning. The AI is a tool in a workflow you govern; the accountability structure you built (charters, tiers, review logs) is what makes response fast instead of panicked.

## Detect — "something's off"

- Anyone can raise it: staff, the manager, or the AI's own self-audit failure. The bar is low on purpose — *"this output looks wrong"* is enough.
- Write down the first-seen time and what was noticed. Screenshot/save the output **before** anything is edited or deleted.
- Check the blast radius question early: **did anything leave the unit?** (sent, posted, printed, acted on) — that decides the next step's urgency.

## Contain — stop the spread, pause the loop

1. **Pause the workflow**: stop the cron, stop using the skill, revert to the manual process. Every Loop Charter names its stop condition and rollback plan — use them.
2. **Correct what's public**: if an erroneous brief/education piece went out, send the correction promptly and plainly ("yesterday's brief misstated X; correct is Y"). Corrections age better than silences.
3. **If the incident touches PHI, personnel information, or anything regulated:** stop self-handling *now* — this leaves unit scope. Route to your organization's privacy/compliance/incident process immediately, exactly as you would for any other privacy event. Your role becomes reporter and witness, not investigator.
4. Preserve the record: the prompt, the output, the charter in force, the review-log trail. The ledger you kept is now your friend.

## Learn — the honest debrief (within a week)

Run it like a good clinical debrief — system lens, no names in the write-up:

- What did we expect the workflow to do? What did it actually do?
- Which gate should have caught this — and why didn't it? (Rubber-stamped approvals? A charter gap? A tier set too low?)
- Fix the *system*: adjust the tier, tighten the gate, amend the charter, or retire the skill. Log the decision and rationale in the decision journal.
- Share the lesson: de-identified, at the next huddle or Steward Council exchange — a unit that surfaces its AI misses teaches every other unit.

## The two lines that keep this honest

- **Never quietly fix and forget.** An uncaptured incident becomes a repeated one.
- **Never let the AI investigate itself.** The debrief is human work; the AI may be asked for its logs, not its verdict.

> Uncertainty is a trigger, not a failure. · Agents propose. Humans judge. Nurses steward.
